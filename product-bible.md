# Founder's Letter

Hardware Radar began with a simple question.

> **"Where can I find the cheapest RAM online?"**

As we explored that question, we discovered something much more important.

People are not really searching for the cheapest RAM.

They are searching for confidence.

Confidence that they are buying the right product.

Confidence that they are not overpaying.

Confidence that the retailer is trustworthy.

Confidence that they are making a smart decision.

That realization changed the direction of this project forever.

Hardware Radar is not simply a price comparison website.

It is a decision-support platform.

Today we begin with RAM.

Tomorrow we may help people purchase every component inside their computer.

Whatever Hardware Radar becomes, one principle should never change.

> **Every feature should help someone buy with confidence.**

If we stay true to that principle, Hardware Radar will always move in the right direction.

— **Mirabelle Labs**

# The Story

Buying computer hardware has become unnecessarily complicated.

People compare dozens of products.

Visit countless retailers.

Watch long review videos.

Read conflicting opinions.

Spend hours researching.

And still wonder if they made the right decision.

Hardware Radar exists to reduce that uncertainty.

Every page should simplify a purchasing decision.

Every recommendation should build confidence.

If a visitor leaves knowing exactly what to buy—and why—we have succeeded.

---

# Mission

> **Help people make better hardware purchasing decisions.**

This sentence defines the entire product.

Whenever uncertainty exists, this mission takes priority.

# Product Philosophy

Hardware Radar is built around one belief.

People do not actually want products.

They want certainty.

They want answers to questions like:

- Is this a good deal?
- Is this compatible?
- Is there something better?
- Should I buy today?
- Can I trust this retailer?
- Am I making the right decision?

Every feature should reduce uncertainty.

---

# Product Principles

## 1. Trust before revenue.

Revenue exists because users trust us.

Never the other way around.

---

## 2. Simplicity scales.

Simple experiences outperform complicated ones.

Every page should answer one question exceptionally well.

---

## 3. Respect attention.

Users should never need twenty tabs open to make one purchase.

---

## 4. Show evidence.

Support recommendations with data whenever possible.

---

## 5. Be transparent.

Explain recommendations.

Explain methodology.

Never hide affiliate relationships.

---

## 6. Build confidence.

Confidence is our product.

Everything else supports it.

---

# The North Star

Every future feature must answer one question.

> **Will this help someone make a better hardware purchasing decision?**

Build it.

Don't.

# Monetization Philosophy

Affiliate commissions are not our product.

Helping people make better purchasing decisions is our product.

Revenue is a consequence of trust.

We will never:

- Recommend inferior products for larger commissions.
- Hide affiliate relationships.
- Create fake scarcity.
- Inflate discounts.
- Manipulate rankings.

Long-term trust is worth more than short-term profit.

# Product Evolution

Hardware Radar begins with RAM.

It will not end there.

Future expansion may include:

- SSDs
- CPUs
- GPUs
- Motherboards
- Power Supplies
- Cases
- Cooling
- Monitors
- Keyboards
- Mice
- Networking

Eventually Hardware Radar evolves into:

> **A hardware intelligence platform that helps people make smarter purchasing decisions.**

Not by adding more products.

By providing better answers.

---

# Things We Will Never Do

We will never:

- Compromise trust for revenue.
- Recommend products we would not recommend ourselves.
- Use deceptive UX.
- Flood pages with intrusive advertising.
- Slow the website with unnecessary effects.
- Build features that do not improve purchasing decisions.
- Chase trends at the expense of the mission.

---

# Founder Commitments

Before building any new feature, ask:

> **Does this help someone buy with confidence?**

If yes—

Build it.

If not—

Don't.

Every major decision should be measured against this question.
